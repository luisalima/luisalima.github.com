You can use AI without understanding it, the way you can drive without knowing
what a piston does — right up until something breaks and you have no model for
why. This series is the model: enough of how AI works, and how it got here, to
reason about it instead of cargo-culting it.

It runs in two arcs.

**Mental models & history** — the orientation, drawn from my "Mental models for
AI" piece:

1. **What a mental model is, and why you need one for AI** — and why the lack of
   one is why so much AI advice is noise.
2. **A (very) brief history of AI** — symbolic → connectionist → statistical ML
   → deep learning → now. The eras rhyme; knowing them tells you what's actually
   new.
3. **How a language model actually works** — the components, and how to *think
   like an LLM*.
4. **What agents are** — putting the "human" loop around the model.

**From scratch** — the mechanics, built by hand so the abstraction is earned:

5. **RAG from first principles** — retrieval, chunking, embeddings, and why the
   naive version disappoints.
6. **MCP from first principles** — what the protocol is once you strip the
   marketing.
7. **Memory, designed** — what "memory" means for an agent, and the choices
   nobody makes on purpose.

Anchored by an essay that's already mostly written; chapters land as I split and
voice it.
